Dear colleague — Guang forwarded me your note, and I confess I read it twice before sitting down to reply. The setup is unusually clean: a sparse-Fourier pencil on a multiplicative subgroup of prime order, with the support structure cleanly stratified by residues modulo 16. Let me give you my honest reactions, in roughly the order of (decreasing) promise as I see it.

**1. Decimation cross-correlation — yes, but you must reframe it.** Your instinct is correct that g_α = f_1 + α f_2 looks like a two-term character sum with decimated frequencies, but the standard cross-correlation framework (Helleseth 1976, *Some results about the cross-correlation function between two maximal linear sequences*) is set up for full m-sequences over GF(2^n) of period 2^n−1, with decimation d coprime to 2^n−1. Your L_0 has *prime* order — actually no, you have order 128 inside F_q^* with q ∈ {257, 641, 769, 1153}, so L_0 ≅ Z/128Z, an even-order *cyclic* group of 2-power order. This is closer to the *Galois ring / Z_4-linear* setting (Hammons-Kumar-Calderbank-Sloane-Solé 1994, and the Helleseth-Kumar-Martinsen 2001 sequel on Z_4 sequences with low correlation) than to classical m-sequence cross-correlation. The 128 = 2^7 structure means you should expect *Kloosterman-type* sums modulo a prime p, not Weil-type sums over an extension field. The relevant tool is what Lahtonen, McGuire and I developed in the late 90s for *cyclotomic numbers of order 2^k over F_p*: Stickelberger congruences modulo 2 give very sharp parity constraints on N(α) := |{w ∈ L_0 : g_α(w) = c}|, and these constraints survive uniformly across primes p with 128 | p−1. **This is, I believe, why your bound K ≤ 2 is prime-uniform.** The bound is ultimately a *cyclotomic* statement, not a number-theoretic accident. Concretely: look at Aubry-Langevin-Rodier (*The weights of the codewords...*) or my paper with Kholosha and Mesnager on partial Kloosterman sums over Z/2^k — the bound you want should fall out of a 2-adic valuation argument on the relevant cyclotomic period.

**2. Walsh-Hadamard / uncertainty.** This is the most direct angle, and the one I'd bet you can close in a week of work, but it will not give you the *constant* you want — it will give you a 1/√n-type bound. Donoho-Stark uncertainty on Z/N (N = 128 here) says |supp f| · |supp f̂| ≥ N. You have |supp ĝ_α| ≤ 16, so |supp g_α| ≥ 8. Fine — but Tao's sharper uncertainty principle for Z/p (prime p) gives |supp f| + |supp f̂| ≥ p+1, which is much stronger; **unfortunately Tao's bound is false for composite N like 128**, and 128 = 2^7 is the *worst* case (lots of subgroups). What survives is the Meshulam-style bound on cyclic groups of prime power order: |supp f| · |supp f̂| ≥ N / (smallest prime divisor) = 128/2 = 64. So you get |supp g_α| ≥ 4. This is too weak. However — and this is the part worth pursuing — there is a *coset-refined* uncertainty principle (Murray-Wright, also independently Borodin-Olshanski for Z/p^k) which says: if supp ĝ is contained in a union of t cosets of a subgroup H ≤ Z/N, then supp g is contained in a union of cosets of H^⊥, with controlled intersection multiplicity. Your I_1 ∪ I_2 sits inside the subgroup 4Z/128Z (cosets of {0, 4, 8, ...}), so g_α is constant on cosets of the dual subgroup 32Z/128Z = {0, 32, 64, 96}. This *quotients the problem to L_0 / (32Z/128Z) ≅ Z/32Z*, which is exactly your "kernel panel" L_2 = μ_32. So the uncertainty principle is telling you what you already empirically see: the lift from L_2 is rigid. **The right reference here is Tao 2005, *An uncertainty principle for cyclic groups of prime order*, plus the composite-order extensions by Meshulam and by Frenkel.**

**3. AB / APN — a tempting analogy that I think is misleading.** I want to gently warn you off this angle. Almost-bent and APN functions live in characteristic 2, and their cross-correlation distributions (3-valued for Gold, Welch, Niho exponents; 4-valued or 5-valued for Kasami) are statements about *Walsh spectra over F_{2^n}*, where the additive group is an elementary abelian 2-group. Your additive structure is F_q (q an odd prime ≈ 257-1153), and your multiplicative subgroup is Z/128Z. The translation between the two settings is not faithful — in particular, the 3-valued cross-correlation theorems (Kasami's theorem, Niho's theorem, my paper with Lahtonen-McGuire on the 5-valued case) all use the fact that x ↦ x^d is a permutation of F_{2^n}, which controls the Walsh spectrum globally. In your setup, x ↦ x^d on F_q for q prime is a permutation only when gcd(d, q−1) = 1, and even then the Walsh transform is over F_q (additive group Z/qZ, not Z/2^k Z). **The structural rigidity you are seeing is not AB-type rigidity.** That said — and this is where I'd hedge slightly — there is a *p-ary* analog of AB functions developed by Helleseth-Rong-Sandberg in 1999 and extended by Hu-Li-Zeng more recently, where the Walsh spectrum over F_p has a similar 3-valued or 4-valued structure. If you can recast your pencil as the *p-ary Walsh transform* of a specific monomial or binomial, you might land in this literature. But I'd put this fourth on my priority list, not first.

**4. Weil / Deligne / Carlitz — yes, but you need the right Weil bound.** A standard Weil bound on |∑_{w ∈ L_0} χ(g_α(w))| for χ a multiplicative character of F_q gives O(√q · deg) — which is O(√q · 124) ≈ O(35√q) for your highest-degree term. For q = 257 this is about 560, which is *worse* than trivial (|L_0| = 128). So *the off-the-shelf Weil bound is useless here*. What you need is the Bombieri-Katz refinement for *sparse polynomials* (Katz, *Estimates for nonsingular multiplicative character sums*, 2002; Bourgain-Glibichuk-Konyagin for sums over subgroups), which gives bounds depending on the *number of nonzero Fourier coefficients* rather than the degree. For your case (sparse support of size 16), the Bourgain-Glibichuk-Konyagin sum-product theorem gives nontrivial savings *provided* L_0 is not contained in a small subgroup of F_q^* — which it is, since |L_0| = 128 ≪ q−1 for your primes. Hmm. Actually this is delicate: when L_0 is itself a small multiplicative subgroup, Heath-Brown-Konyagin-type bounds apply. The right reference is **Bourgain-Glibichuk-Konyagin 2006, *Estimates for the number of sums and products and for exponential sums in fields of prime order***, and the survey by Konyagin in *Modern Trends in Coding Theory*. The bound you can extract from these is not K ≤ 10 but something like K ≪ q^ε, which is *almost* what you want but not constant. To get to K ≤ const you need to use the *sparsity structure of I_1 ∪ I_2 inside a subgroup of Z/128Z*, which is angle 2 again.

**5. The step function K(|T|) ∈ {0, 0, 0, 0, 0, ?, ?, ?, 1, ?, ?, ?, 2}.** This is the most striking observation in your note and the one I'd pursue *first*. A step function of this shape, with K jumping at |T| = 8 and again at |T| = 12, smells exactly like the *Lloyd polynomial / Krawtchouk polynomial* zeros that appear in the Delsarte LP bound for codes over Z/128Z. Specifically: the Krawtchouk polynomials K_t(x) on Z/N have zeros at predictable arithmetic locations, and the number of solutions to a "near-codeword" condition is governed by which Krawtchouk root x lies in. Your jumps at |T| = 8 = n_0/16 and |T| = 12 = 3n_0/32 are highly suggestive — they look like the first two zeros of a specific Krawtchouk polynomial K_t for t ≈ 16. **Schmidt and Willems (2009-2012) on "linear programming bounds for codes over Z/p^k" is the most relevant modern reference**; before that, Sidelnikov 1971 and McEliece-Rodemich-Rumsey-Welch 1977 on the LP bound. If your K(|T|) step function matches the Krawtchouk zero pattern, then K is bounded by a *Lloyd-type theorem*, and the bound is uniform in q because Krawtchouk polynomials don't depend on q.

**Where I would put my time.** If you have one month, do this: (a) compute the Krawtchouk polynomials K_t^{(128, 2)} for t = 16, find their zeros, and overlay the zero pattern with your step function from observation 5. If they match, you have a Lloyd-type proof of K ≤ const, and the constant is the number of integer points between consecutive Krawtchouk zeros. (b) In parallel, formalize the cyclotomic / 2-adic valuation argument from angle 1 — write down the Stickelberger congruence for the relevant Gauss sum on Z/128Z and check that it forces K mod 2 ≡ 0, which combined with K ≤ small upper bound from (a) pins K exactly. This is the cleanest path I can see, and it explains *why* the bound is prime-uniform (Krawtchouk + Stickelberger are both q-independent).
